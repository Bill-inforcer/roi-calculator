Field Guide

What each input means, where to find it, and how to read the answer

← Back to calculator

Every input in the calculator has three things to think through: what it is (the precise definition), where to find it (where the partner pulls the number from), and how to read it (what their value tells you about their current state). Use this guide as a quick reference during a QBR, or hand it to a partner who wants to take the model home.

1. Partner business profile

Number of managed tenants

What it is

The total count of end-customer organisations (tenants) the MSP manages — not users, not seats. One tenant = one customer company.

Where to find it

The customer or tenant count in their PSA (ConnectWise Manage, Autotask, HaloPSA), or the count of M365 tenants in their partner portal.

How to read it

Under 30 tenants suggests a small MSP, 30–100 is mid-market, 100+ is larger. The tenant count on its own isn't the headline — it's the combination of tenants and seats that tells you average customer size, which signals the kind of clients they serve.

Total managed seats (users)

What it is

The total billable end-user count across every tenant combined.

Where to find it

Sum of M365 licence seats across all tenants in their partner portal, or the seat-count line on their PSA invoicing report.

How to read it

This is the most important number in the model — nearly every dollar of uplift flows from it. Take time to confirm it before moving on. If the partner says "around 2,000," push for the actual seat count from their billing system. A 10% error here ripples through every output.

Average revenue per seat per month (PEPM)

What it is

PEPM = Per End-user Per Month. The total monthly managed-services revenue divided by the total seat count. This is the MSP industry's standard ARPU measure.

Where to find it

Monthly recurring revenue (MRR) ÷ total seats. If the partner doesn't track this, calculate it on the spot — they'll know their MRR and you already have their seat count.

How to read it

Industry context: $50 PEPM is transactional or break-fix-adjacent; $80–120 PEPM is a healthy modern managed-services stack; $150+ is security-rich or a specialist vertical. If the partner is below $80, that's your opening — inforcer's pitch is that a $3–5 PEPM uplift for enhanced M365 security is credible.

2. Current-state P&L

Cost of services (COGS) — % of revenue

What it is

The direct cost of delivering the service, expressed as a percentage of revenue. Mostly engineer labour charged to service-desk work, plus vendor licence pass-through.

Where to find it

P&L "Cost of Sales" or "Cost of Services" total ÷ total revenue. In Xero or QuickBooks, this is the COGS account total. The partner's accountant will have it ready.

How to read it

Service Leadership's benchmark for a healthy MSP is COGS in the 35–45% range. Above 50% means labour intensity is eating profitability — usually under-priced contracts or low automation. Below 30% is either very efficient or under-investing in delivery (watch for quality problems downstream).

Operating expenses (Opex) — % of revenue

What it is

Everything that isn't direct service delivery — sales, marketing, G&A, owner compensation, rent, internal software (CRM/PSA/RMM), office, training, travel.

Where to find it

P&L "Operating Expenses" total ÷ total revenue.

How to read it

Healthy MSPs run 35–50% Opex. Higher than 55% is usually either heavy sales/marketing investment (fine if they're growing fast) or owner-comp drag (a yellow flag — talk about it carefully). COGS + Opex combined tells you what EBITDA margin is left, which the model calculates next.

3. Current M365 security stack

M365 security tools — combined cost per seat / month

What it is

The total per-seat monthly spend on every M365-security-adjacent tool the partner pays for today — third-party CIPP wrappers, security reporting, baseline-configuration tools, conditional-access managers, etc.

Where to find it

Sum the per-seat costs of each vendor in their security toolbelt for an average tenant.

How to read it

Most MSPs sit at $5–15 per seat per month across three to five tools. Above $20 means there's a stack-rationalisation story before you even get to the PEPM uplift. Under $3 means they're probably under-invested — the conversation is upsell, not rationalisation.

Engineer hours / month on M365 security admin

What it is

The total hours engineers spend each month on manual M365 security work across all tenants — baseline checks, CA rule changes, licence reviews, security incident response, monthly customer security reports.

Where to find it

PSA time tracking with category filters, ticket-category reports, or the partner's gut estimate.

How to read it

Partners almost always underestimate this on the first pass — actual hours are typically 1.5–2x their first guess. Probe specifically: hours on baseline reviews, on CA rule tweaks, on monthly security reports, on incident response. 60–100 hours/month is typical for a 50–100-tenant MSP.

Loaded engineer cost / hour

What it is

The fully-loaded hourly cost of an engineer — salary + super + benefits + overhead allocation, divided by billable hours.

Where to find it

From HR or finance, or the rule of thumb: annual salary package × 1.3 ÷ ~1,800 billable hours per year.

How to read it

AU/NZ ranges: $80–100/hr for L1, $100–130/hr for L2, $130–180/hr for senior engineers. The default of $90 is a blended L1/L2 mid-point. Use the partner's actual blended rate if they have it.

4. inforcer pricing & uplift

inforcer cost per seat / month

What it is

The partner's negotiated PEPM price for inforcer.

Where to find it

The partner agreement or the pricing sheet you've shared with them.

How to read it

This is a known input — use whatever's contracted. If there's a pricing-discount conversation, that's a separate negotiation; don't quietly model a discount inside the calculator without flagging it.

PEPM uplift the MSP can charge end customers

What it is

The dollar amount the partner adds to its own PEPM when packaging inforcer into a re-branded "enhanced M365 security" offering to its end customers.

Where to find it

Agree it jointly with the partner — based on what they think their customers will accept.

How to read it

$3–5 PEPM is standard. $4 is the conservative default. $7+ is aggressive — only credible if the partner has a strong security brand and serves verticals with regulatory drivers (legal, financial services, healthcare).

Year-1 attach rate (% of seats)

What it is

The percentage of total seats the partner expects to roll the new service onto in the first 12 months.

Where to find it

A partner judgement call, grounded in their confidence about customer-side comms.

How to read it

50–70% is realistic for a partner with a strong customer base and a clear rollout plan. Below 30% means they're either under-confident or haven't planned the customer comms. Above 80% is unrealistic without a forced-migration play.

Retired tools — per-seat saving

What it is

The combined per-seat licence cost of every tool the partner agrees to actively retire because inforcer replaces it.

Where to find it

From the stack-rationalisation conversation. Only count tools the partner commits to turning off — not "tools they might consider retiring later."

How to read it

$2–5 PEPM in retired-tool savings is typical. Zero is fine if the partner says they're not retiring anything — the upside then comes purely from PEPM uplift and engineer time saved. Don't pad this number.

Engineer hours saved / month via automation

What it is

Hours per month the partner expects to recover across the engineering team once inforcer automates the manual M365 security work captured above.

Where to find it

A reduction from the "engineer hours / month on M365 security admin" field. Aim for an honest, agreed estimate.

How to read it

40–60% reduction is what most partners report by months 3–6 once they're using inforcer fluently. The default of 40 hours (against 80 hours current) is a conservative 50%. If a partner says zero, they probably don't believe automation will move the needle — pause the conversation there before continuing.

Churn reduction (Yr 1, percentage points)

What it is

The percentage points of customer churn the partner thinks the new service will avoid in Year 1, because customers paying for a security wrapper are stickier.

Where to find it

Partner judgement, ideally cross-checked against their actual churn rate.

How to read it

0.5–1.5 percentage points is sensible. The maths: if the partner is currently churning 8% per year and inforcer brings it to 7%, that's a 1pp improvement and ~1% of revenue retained. This is the easiest place for a sceptical partner to push back — that's fine; dial it to zero if they don't believe it, and the rest of the model still stands.

5. Valuation

EV / EBITDA multiple

What it is

The multiple a buyer in an M&A scenario would pay for the MSP, applied to its annual EBITDA. This is the standard valuation metric for service businesses.

Where to find it

The partner's M&A advisor, recent peer comparables, or industry reports. The MSP peer range sits at 5–10x.

How to read it

5–6x indicates smaller or less-scalable MSPs; 7–9x is a healthy mid-market MSP; 10x+ is best-in-class or strategic-acquirer territory. The 2024 industry average sat around 7.5–8.5x (Service Leadership). The default of 8x is conservative-realistic. A note of care: M&A-readiness conversations can backfire with owners who feel "groomed for exit" — lead with valuation literacy, not transaction intent.

Putting it together

The model multiplies the four uplift drivers — PEPM uplift, stack rationalisation, engineer time freed, and churn reduction — through the partner's own P&L structure to produce an annual EBITDA improvement. That number, multiplied by their EV/EBITDA multiple, becomes equity value created.

Every input is the partner's number. If they push back on one, dial it down or zero it out and watch the result update live. That's the point: it's not a vendor average, it's their model.
